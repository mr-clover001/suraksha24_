One of the most common questions families ask us at Suraksha24 is deceptively simple: "Does my parent need a nurse, or just someone to be with them?" The answer shapes cost, care quality, and outcomes — so it's worth understanding the real difference.

## What companion care covers

Companion care focuses on emotional wellbeing and daily living support:

- Conversation and social engagement
- Help with meals, light housekeeping, and errands
- Accompaniment to appointments or walks
- Medication reminders (not administration)
- General supervision for safety

This is ideal for seniors who are largely independent but shouldn't be alone all day — especially those at risk of isolation, mild memory lapses, or minor mobility limitations.

## What skilled nursing care covers

Skilled nursing involves clinical, hands-on medical care:

- Wound dressing, injections, catheter or tube care
- Vitals monitoring and medication administration
- Post-surgical or chronic illness management
- Coordination with doctors on treatment plans

This is necessary when there's an active medical condition, recent hospitalization, or a diagnosis requiring ongoing clinical attention.

## The grey area — and why it matters

Many families choose companion care because it's more affordable, when a parent's condition actually needs clinical oversight. This can be dangerous — missed medication timing or unnoticed symptoms can lead to hospital readmission.

Conversely, some families over-hire nursing care for a parent who mainly needs company and structure, at higher cost than necessary.

<Callout type="tip" title="How to decide">
  Ask: is there an active medical condition requiring monitoring or intervention? If yes, nursing care is essential. If the core need is safety, structure, and emotional presence, companion care is likely sufficient — and can always be upgraded later.
</Callout>
