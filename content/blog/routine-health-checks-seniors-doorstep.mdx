Preventive health monitoring is one of the most effective, and most neglected, tools in senior care. Many serious conditions are caught late simply because routine checks weren't done regularly — not because the technology or tests weren't available. Here's what every senior should be tracking, and how often.

## Blood pressure — every visit, ideally weekly at home

Hypertension is often called a "silent" condition because it rarely shows symptoms until it causes damage. Regular monitoring, even with a simple home device, catches dangerous trends early.

## Blood sugar — every 3 months, or more often if diabetic

For seniors with diabetes or prediabetes, fasting and post-prandial glucose tracking helps catch dangerous highs or lows before they become emergencies, and helps physicians adjust medication accurately.

## Complete blood count — every 6 months

This basic test can reveal anemia, infection, or early signs of more serious blood conditions — often before any visible symptoms appear.

## Kidney and liver function — every 6–12 months

Especially important for seniors on long-term medications, since many drugs are processed through these organs and dosage may need adjustment as function changes with age.

## Thyroid function — annually

Thyroid imbalances are common in older adults and often mistaken for normal aging — fatigue, weight changes, and mood shifts can all trace back to an undiagnosed thyroid issue.

## Vitamin D and B12 — annually

Deficiencies are extremely common among seniors, particularly those with limited sun exposure or restricted diets, and are linked to fatigue, bone weakness, and cognitive symptoms.

## Bone density (DEXA scan) — every 1–2 years for at-risk patients

Particularly important for postmenopausal women and anyone with a history of fractures, to catch osteoporosis before a fall causes serious injury.

## ECG and cardiac screening — annually, or as advised

Especially critical for seniors with a family history of heart disease or existing risk factors like hypertension and diabetes.
