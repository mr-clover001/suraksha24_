A hip fracture is one of the most feared injuries in older age — not just because of the fracture itself, but because of what can follow if recovery isn't managed properly: loss of independence, muscle wasting, and in serious cases, long-term immobility. With structured physiotherapy, most seniors can regain significant mobility. Here's a realistic timeline.

## Week 1–2: Post-surgical stabilization

Most hip fractures require surgical fixation or replacement. In the first two weeks, physiotherapy focuses on gentle range-of-motion exercises, safe positioning to avoid pressure sores, and early supervised movement — often just sitting up or standing briefly with support.

## Week 2–6: Guided weight-bearing

Depending on the surgeon's clearance, this phase introduces walking with a frame or walker, careful weight-bearing exercises, and continued strength work for the unaffected leg and core, which compensates during recovery.

## Week 6–12: Building independence

This is typically when patients transition from a walker to a cane, and eventually to unaided walking for short distances. Balance training becomes central here, since fear of falling again can be as limiting as the physical injury itself.

## Month 3–6: Full functional recovery

Most seniors regain most of their pre-injury mobility by this stage, though full strength recovery — especially for those over 75 — can take up to a year. Ongoing physiotherapy at this stage focuses on stamina, stairs, and confidence in daily movement.

## Why timeline varies so much

Age, pre-existing conditions like osteoporosis or diabetes, nutrition, and — critically — how consistently physiotherapy is followed all affect recovery speed. Patients who skip sessions or rest too much often recover slower, not faster.

<Callout type="tip" title="The psychological side">
  Fear of falling again is extremely common after a hip fracture and can quietly sabotage recovery if not addressed. A good physiotherapist works on confidence, not just muscles.
</Callout>
