Falls rarely happen because of one big hazard. They happen because of several small ones, stacked together, in rooms your parents have lived in for decades and no longer really "see." Here's a practical room-by-room checklist you can talk through on your next call — or ask a caregiver to check in person.

## Bedroom

- Is there a clear, well-lit path from the bed to the bathroom?
- Is a lamp or light switch reachable without getting out of bed?
- Are loose bedside rugs removed or taped down?
- Is the bed height comfortable to get in and out of without straining?

## Bathroom

The bathroom is the single most common place for falls at home, usually because of wet floors and nothing to hold onto.

- Grab bars near the toilet and inside the shower/bathing area
- A non-slip mat both inside and just outside the shower
- A stable plastic stool for bathing, if standing for long is tiring
- Good lighting, including a night light for after-dark trips

<Callout type="info" title="Good to know">
  Simple additions like grab bars and non-slip mats are inexpensive and can usually be
  installed within a day — this is often the very first thing a home visit identifies.
</Callout>

## Kitchen

- Frequently used items stored between waist and shoulder height, avoiding step stools
- Spills wiped immediately, not left "for later"
- A sturdy chair nearby for tasks that take a while, like chopping

## Living Room & Common Areas

- Clear walking paths, with cables and cords tucked away
- Furniture with stable armrests to push up from
- Non-slip backing on any loose rugs, or removing them entirely
- A phone kept within reach, not across the room

## Stairs & Entryways

- Handrails on both sides where possible
- Non-slip strips on the edge of each step
- Outdoor steps checked for moss, cracks or uneven surfaces after rain

> "Families are often surprised how many of these fixes cost very little — the hard part is usually just being physically present to notice them."

## If a fall has already happened

A fall is also a signal, not just an accident to clean up after. It's worth asking a professional to review overall mobility, medication (some medicines affect balance), and eyesight, rather than treating it as a one-off.
