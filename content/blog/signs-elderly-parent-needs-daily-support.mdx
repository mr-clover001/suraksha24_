A phone call can tell you a lot — and hide a lot. "I'm fine, beta" is often true. Sometimes it isn't. When you're thousands of miles away, the signs that a parent needs more daily support tend to show up in small, easy-to-miss details rather than one dramatic moment.

Here are five worth paying attention to.

## 1. The kitchen tells a story

Ask what they cooked today, not just whether they ate. Skipped meals, the same simple dish on repeat, or a fridge that hasn't been restocked in a while can point to fatigue, difficulty standing for long, or reduced appetite — all worth a closer look.

## 2. Medication routines slip

A missed dose here and there happens to everyone. A pattern of confusion about which pill goes with which meal, or medicines still sitting in last week's organiser, is a different signal — one that a structured routine and a second pair of eyes can usually fix quickly.

<Callout type="info" title="Good to know">
  A trained caregiver's first visit typically starts with exactly this — mapping the current
  medication routine and flagging anything that needs a doctor's attention.
</Callout>

## 3. The home itself looks different

Unopened post piling up, dust in places that were always spotless, or laundry left longer than usual are rarely about neglect — they're usually about energy. Ask yourself: would this have been true five years ago?

## 4. Mobility changes they don't mention

Parents rarely volunteer "I'm scared of falling." Watch instead for indirect clues: furniture rearranged to create handholds, reluctance to go up to the terrace, or a walking stick that's suddenly always nearby.

## 5. Mood shifts that outlast a bad day

Everyone has an off day. A parent who sounds flat, uninterested in calls, or repeats worries more than usual over several weeks may be dealing with loneliness or low mood — one of the most common and most treatable things a regular in-person visit can help with.

> "The biggest relief for families isn't a diagnosis — it's simply having someone who visits regularly and would notice if something changed."

## What to do next

None of these signs on their own mean an emergency. Together, and over time, they're worth a proper conversation — ideally with a professional who can do an in-home assessment rather than guess over the phone.

That's the starting point for most families who reach out to us: not a crisis, just a growing sense that a phone call isn't enough anymore.
