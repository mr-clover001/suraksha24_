Every family has a version of this moment: a parent forgets a name mid-sentence, repeats a story they told last week, or misplaces their glasses for the third time this month. Most of the time, this is ordinary ageing. Sometimes, it's the beginning of something that benefits from an early conversation with a doctor. Telling the difference from another country, over occasional phone calls, is genuinely hard.

## What's usually just ordinary ageing

- Occasionally forgetting a name, then recalling it later
- Misplacing items sometimes, but retracing steps to find them
- Taking a little longer to learn something new
- Momentary distraction during a conversation

## Patterns worth a closer look

- Forgetting recently learned information more frequently, and not recalling it later even with hints
- Getting confused about the time, date, or where they are
- Repeating the same questions within a short span, even after being answered
- Difficulty following a familiar recipe, route, or routine they've done for years
- Withdrawing from conversations, hobbies or social plans they used to enjoy

<Callout type="info" title="Good to know">
  None of this is a diagnosis, and it shouldn't be treated like one. It's a starting point for
  a conversation with a doctor, who can properly assess what's going on.
</Callout>

## Why an in-person view matters more than a phone call

Memory and mood changes are easy to miss over a call — a parent can sound completely normal for ten minutes and still be struggling day-to-day. This is one of the clearest cases where a regular in-person visit, and a caregiver trained to notice these patterns, adds something a phone call simply can't.

> "Families often tell us the same thing: 'On the phone, she sounds exactly like herself.' That's exactly why someone needs to actually be there."

## What a first step can look like

1. Have an honest, gentle conversation with your parent — not an interrogation.
2. Arrange a check-up with their regular doctor, sharing specific examples rather than general worry.
3. Consider a companion caregiver for regular in-person contact and observation, even before any diagnosis.
4. Keep a simple shared note of specific incidents (dates, what happened) to share with the doctor — patterns are more useful than a single anecdote.

## A note on tone

It's easy for these conversations to feel frightening. Most changes turn out to be manageable, and even when they're not, an early, calm response is almost always better than a delayed, panicked one.
