Most NRI families don't plan for elder care in advance — they react to a fall, a diagnosis, or a worrying phone call, usually from a different time zone, usually at an inconvenient hour. It doesn't have to start that way. Here's a calmer way to approach it.

## Step 1: Start with an honest assessment, not a panic decision

Before choosing any service, get a clear picture of what your parents actually need day-to-day: medication management, companionship, mobility support, or ongoing nursing care. A proper in-home assessment (many providers, including us, offer this free) is far more useful than guessing from abroad.

## Step 2: Decide what "being informed" means to you

Some families want a detailed report after every visit. Others just want to know someone will call if anything changes. Be specific about this early — it shapes which provider is the right fit, and avoids frustration later.

<Callout type="tip" title="Ask this on the first call">
  "If something goes wrong at 2 a.m. India time, who calls me, and how quickly?" A confident,
  specific answer tells you more than any brochure.
</Callout>

## Step 3: Understand who you're actually talking to

Ask whether you'll have one dedicated point of contact, or whether every call goes to a general helpline. For ongoing care, a named care manager who knows your parents' case is usually worth prioritising over a slightly lower price.

## Step 4: Sort out payment from abroad early

Don't leave this until an emergency. Ask specifically:

- Do they accept international cards or bank transfers?
- Can they invoice in a way your bank abroad will accept?
- Is pricing transparent upfront, with no hidden per-visit surprises?

## Step 5: Agree on what happens in an emergency — before you need it

This is the conversation families most regret skipping. Ask exactly what happens if your parent needs an ambulance at night: who is dispatched, who coordinates hospital admission, and who stays with your parent until family can be reached or arrives.

> "We tell every family the same thing: don't wait for the emergency to ask these questions. Ask them on day one."

## Step 6: Set a review rhythm

Care needs change. A short check-in every few months — even just a call with the care manager — keeps the plan aligned with how your parents are actually doing, not how they were doing when you first set things up.

## A simpler starting point

If this feels like a lot to organise from another country, that's exactly the gap a dedicated care manager is meant to close — one call to start, one person coordinating everything after that.
